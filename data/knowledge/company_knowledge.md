## Financial Accounting Concepts and Standards

### Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP)

GAAP refers to the standard framework of guidelines for financial accounting used primarily in the United States. Key principles include the revenue recognition principle (recognize revenue when earned), the matching principle (match expenses to the revenues they generate), the full disclosure principle (disclose all relevant financial information), and the historical cost principle (record assets at original cost).

### International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)

IFRS is the international accounting standard framework used in over 140 countries including the UK, Australia, and most of Europe. Key standards relevant to Global FPO clients include IFRS 15 (revenue recognition), IFRS 16 (leases), IFRS 9 (financial instruments), and IAS 36 (impairment of assets).

### Chart of Accounts

A chart of accounts is the organized list of all accounts used by a business to record financial transactions. Standard categories include assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, and expenses. Global FPO sets up and maintains chart of accounts structures tailored to each client's industry and reporting requirements.

### Accounts Payable and Accounts Receivable

Accounts payable (AP) represents money owed by the business to suppliers and vendors for goods and services received. Accounts receivable (AR) represents money owed to the business by customers for goods and services delivered. Managing AP and AR effectively is critical to cash flow management.

### Bank Reconciliation

Bank reconciliation is the process of matching the transactions in a company's accounting records to the corresponding entries on the bank statement. Reconciling items include outstanding checks, deposits in transit, bank fees, and errors. Global FPO performs bank reconciliation as part of standard bookkeeping services.

### Accruals and Deferrals

Accruals are revenues earned or expenses incurred that have not yet been recorded. Deferrals are revenues received or expenses paid in advance that have not yet been earned or incurred. Both are recorded via journal entries as part of the month-end close process to ensure financial statements reflect the correct period's activity.

### Depreciation

Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible fixed asset over its useful life. Common methods include straight-line depreciation (equal amounts each period), declining balance (accelerating expense in early years), and units of production (based on output). Depreciation reduces the book value of assets on the balance sheet and is recorded as an expense on the income statement.

### Financial Ratios

Key financial ratios used in analysis include:
- **Current ratio:** Current assets divided by current liabilities. Measures short-term liquidity.
- **Gross margin:** Gross profit divided by revenue. Measures profitability before operating expenses.
- **Net profit margin:** Net income divided by revenue. Measures overall profitability.
- **Debt-to-equity ratio:** Total debt divided by total equity. Measures financial leverage.
- **Days sales outstanding (DSO):** Average accounts receivable divided by average daily revenue. Measures collection efficiency.
- **Days payable outstanding (DPO):** Average accounts payable divided by average daily cost of goods sold. Measures payment timing.

## Invoice Management

### What Is an Invoice

An invoice is a commercial document issued by a seller to a buyer indicating the goods or services provided, the quantities, agreed prices, payment terms, and total amount due. Invoices serve as the primary document for accounts receivable management and revenue recognition.

### Invoice Fields

A standard invoice includes the following fields:
- Invoice number (unique identifier)
- Invoice date (date of issue)
- Posting date (date posted to accounting records)
- Customer name
- Customer UUID or customer ID
- Ledger name (the accounting ledger the invoice is posted to)
- Line items (product or service name, quantity, unit price)
- Subtotal (sum of line items before tax)
- Tax amount (applicable sales tax or VAT)
- Total amount (subtotal plus tax)
- Status (Draft, Pending, Approved, Posted, Paid, Cancelled)
- Payment terms (e.g. NET-30, NET-60, due on receipt)
- Due date

### Invoice Statuses

- **Draft:** Invoice has been created but not yet submitted for approval or sent to the customer.
- **Pending:** Invoice has been submitted and is awaiting approval.
- **Approved:** Invoice has been reviewed and approved internally.
- **Posted:** Invoice has been recorded in the accounting ledger.
- **Paid:** Payment has been received and applied.
- **Cancelled:** Invoice has been voided and is no longer active.

### Invoice Approval Workflow

Standard invoice approval for high-value transactions requires manager review before posting. The specific approval threshold varies by client configuration. Invoices above the threshold follow a maker-checker workflow where the preparer submits and a reviewer approves before the invoice is posted to the ledger.

### Credit Notes

A credit note (also called a credit memo) is issued to reduce or cancel an existing invoice, typically due to returns, errors, or agreed discounts after invoicing. Credit notes reference the original invoice number and reduce the outstanding accounts receivable balance.

## Payroll Concepts

### Payroll Processing Cycle

The payroll processing cycle includes collecting timekeeping data, calculating gross pay, applying deductions (tax withholdings, benefits, garnishments), calculating net pay, processing payments (direct deposit or check), filing payroll tax returns, and maintaining payroll records.

### Payroll Tax Obligations

Employers are responsible for withholding and remitting employee income taxes, Social Security and Medicare taxes (FICA in the USA), state and local income taxes where applicable, and unemployment taxes (FUTA and SUTA in the USA). Employer payroll tax obligations vary by jurisdiction.

### Year-End Payroll

Year-end payroll tasks include reconciling payroll records to tax filings, preparing W-2 forms (USA) or P60 forms (UK) for employees, preparing 1099 forms for contractors, filing year-end payroll tax returns, and reviewing for any corrections needed.

## Tax Filing Concepts

### US Federal Tax

US businesses file federal income tax returns using Form 1120 (C corporations), Form 1120-S (S corporations), or Schedule C attached to Form 1040 (sole proprietors). Partnerships file Form 1065. Federal income tax returns are generally due by the 15th day of the fourth month after the fiscal year end (April 15 for calendar year filers), with extensions available.

### US State Tax

Most US states impose their own income tax on businesses operating within their borders. Nexus (sufficient connection to a state to be subject to its tax) may be established by physical presence, economic activity, or payroll. Multi-state businesses must apportion income among states using state-specific formulas.

### UK Tax

UK companies pay Corporation Tax on their profits. The rate and filing deadlines are set by HMRC. VAT (Value Added Tax) is charged at the standard rate on most goods and services, with reduced and zero rates applying to certain categories. VAT returns are filed quarterly or monthly depending on the scheme.

### Australian Tax

Australian companies pay corporate income tax at rates set by the Australian Taxation Office (ATO). GST (Goods and Services Tax) is a 10% broad-based tax on most goods and services, with BAS (Business Activity Statement) filed monthly or quarterly. Payroll tax is a state-level obligation in Australia.

### GST and VAT

GST and VAT are consumption taxes collected by businesses on behalf of the government. Businesses charge GST/VAT on sales (output tax) and can claim credits for GST/VAT paid on purchases (input tax). The net amount payable is output tax minus input tax credits. Registration thresholds vary by country.

## Audit Concepts

### Types of Audits

**External audit:** An independent examination of financial statements by a registered auditor to express an opinion on whether the statements present a true and fair view in accordance with applicable accounting standards.

**Internal audit:** An independent, objective assurance and consulting activity designed to add value and improve an organization's operations. Internal audits evaluate risk management, control, and governance processes.

**Tax audit:** A review by a tax authority of an organization's tax returns and supporting records to verify compliance with tax laws.

**Compliance audit:** An examination of an organization's adherence to regulatory requirements, contractual obligations, or internal policies.

### Audit Evidence

Auditors gather evidence through inspection of documents and records, observation of processes, inquiry of management and staff, confirmation from third parties (e.g. bank confirmations, debtor confirmations), recalculation of figures, and analytical procedures (comparing expected vs actual results).

### Internal Controls

Internal controls are policies and procedures designed to provide reasonable assurance that financial reporting is reliable, operations are effective and efficient, and the organization complies with applicable laws and regulations. Key control types include authorization controls, segregation of duties, reconciliation controls, physical safeguards, and IT access controls.

## Glossary of Financial Terms

**Accrual accounting:** An accounting method where revenue and expenses are recorded when earned or incurred, regardless of when cash is received or paid.

**Amortization:** The gradual reduction of a debt or intangible asset value over time through scheduled payments or periodic write-downs.

**Balance sheet:** A financial statement showing a company's assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time.

**Capital expenditure (CapEx):** Spending on acquiring or improving long-term assets such as property, plant, and equipment.

**Cash basis accounting:** An accounting method where revenue and expenses are recorded only when cash is received or paid.

**Cost of goods sold (COGS):** The direct costs attributable to the production or purchase of goods sold by a company.

**Depreciation:** The systematic allocation of a tangible asset's cost over its useful life.

**EBITDA:** Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. A measure of operating performance.

**Fixed assets:** Long-term tangible assets used in operations, such as buildings, machinery, and vehicles.

**General ledger:** The master record of all financial transactions of a business, organized by account.

**Gross profit:** Revenue minus cost of goods sold.

**Journal entry:** A record of a financial transaction in the accounting system, showing the accounts debited and credited.

**Ledger:** A collection of accounts used to record transactions of a specific type.

**Net income:** Revenue minus all expenses including COGS, operating expenses, interest, and taxes.

**Operating expenses (OpEx):** Ongoing costs of running a business that are not directly tied to production, such as rent, salaries, and utilities.

**Purchase order (PO):** A formal document issued by a buyer to a seller authorizing a purchase at an agreed price and quantity.

**Reconciliation:** The process of ensuring two sets of records (such as accounting records and bank statements) are in agreement.

**Revenue recognition:** The accounting principle governing when and how revenue is recorded in the financial statements.

**Trial balance:** A report listing all accounts and their balances at a specific date, used to verify that debits equal credits.

**Working capital:** Current assets minus current liabilities. A measure of short-term financial health and operational liquidity.
